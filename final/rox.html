The Struggle to Define Dudley

As the city works to capture some of Boston's development boom, residents in Roxbury are worried they won't have a hand in shaping the area's future.

VERNA IS RE-ROOTED

Verna Hampton was raised in Roxbury, with southern roots in Georgia. As a performer, she’s traveled all over the world, and has come and gone from her hometown several times over the last years. She remembers Woolworth’s and Blair’s, and a handful of other stores she’d visit, sometimes riding in with her grandmother. She can describe the vertigo of turning the corner on the old Elevated Orange Line that once went straight through Dudley Square.

“It was like a ride at Great Adventure, it was scary. You said a second little Hail Mary,” she recalls. “You’d go right past there and you’d say, ‘Oh gosh, are we going to fall into the movie theater today?’”

That movie theater is now a parking lot, and the Orange Line was moved about a half-mile west of where it used to be. These are just a few of the many changes that have gone on in Roxbury and Dudley Square over the years — changes that Hampton would notice every time she came back from another stint performing in New York City or beyond.

“It was a lot different. It was a bustling, exciting, economic gathering of people coming to shop, to have fun,” she says. “There were so many things that disappeared. And I don’t know if it’s for the better.”

Hampton is one of many Roxbury natives who are questioning the changes taking place in the neighborhood at a time when a thriving real estate market makes its mark on areas throughout Boston — and in just about every major urban market in America. These anxieties were on display at a December 1st meeting hosted by the Boston Planning and Development Agency. Residents and community members filled the room to capacity. The purpose was to present plans for Roxbury's Parcel 3, but things quickly turned chaotic, and the meeting ended before the presentations were complete.

Dudley's Changing Demographics

For decades, Roxbury has been a majority black neighborhood. The neighborhood's past is inseparable from Boston's racial justice history. Dudley Square is at the heart of the neighborhood and in many ways reflects what's going on all over Roxbury. And as the land in Dudley Square becomes more valuable and the city begins to develop a handful of parcels that have been vacant for decades, residents are experiencing a demographic shift.
